Abstract

Specialized knowledge is often wielded as a tool of empire. In the case of the Abbasid Caliphate, which saw large explosion of intellectual activity in a variety of fields during its existence, it was both the practical application of knowledge and the act of creating it that were utilized in the imperial sense.  This paper examines Al-Khwarizmi’s Al-jabr, a foundational work in the creation of the mathematical field of algebra, and its creation within the uniquely imperial reign of Caliph Al-Mamun. Through an analysis of the biography of Al-Khwarizmi, the context of the creation of the work, the importance of religion in Islam and specific political decisions of Al-Mamun, the intricate connections between mathematics and empire are traced.

Introduction

In many canonical introductions to the history of mathematics begin with the works of Greek thinkers and proceed to skip ahead to post-Renaissance Europe[1].  The contributions often glossed over come from one of the richest traditions of intellectual activity in human history, the Golden Age of Islam. The Abbasid Caliphate, the imperial locale for this important period, saw an explosion in the literary and scientific activity during this time. Still, when the Abbasids are recognized in academic literature, it is often the mass translations of Classical work that is alluded to. However, outside of that very important conversion of literature, the Abbasids lay claim to a host of novel intellectual discovery, including the founding of the mathematical field of algebra. The creation of this foundational mathematical work within this Abbasid caliphate, an exceptionally large and diverse land empire, creates a unique environment to study the ways in which the development of knowledge can be used for imperial power. Additionally, using mathematics as the focus of the analysis allows for an exploration of how a normatively insular academic field can still have profoundly important interactions with human culture.

A Brief History of the Abbasid Caliphate

The Abbasid Caliphate was one of the early Islamic caliphates, coming to formation around a century after the death of the Prophet Muhammad in 632 AD. The Abbasid’s imperial predecessor was the Umayyad Caliphate, for which disdain grew greatly towards the middle of the eighth century. Due to political mismanagement and exclusionary religious policies, this disdain morphed into the cause of the Abbasid Revolution[2]. Abul ‘Abbas al-Saffah claimed the title caliph on the ground of his relation to Muhammad through Muhammad’s uncle Abbas. Under Caliph al-Mansur, al-Saffah’s successor, the capital city of the caliphate moved to Baghdad, the city that was to become the intellectual center of the Islamic Golden Age.

During these early years of the new caliphate, the new caliphs definitively differentiated themselves from the Umayyads through a prominent demonstration of religious tolerance towards non-Muslim subjects (dhimmis). Although there were certain taxes levied on these populations, the government recognized the validity of their religions and they were not segregated from the Muslim population[3].

In 786 AD, Harun al-Rashid ascended as caliph. During his reign, Baghdad grew as a hub of intellectual activity. Al-Rashid remains popular in modern times because of his depiction in famous collection of folklore One Thousand and One Nights. In addition to this literary fame, Rashid was responsible for the temporary political disintegration of the caliphate. In avoiding creating a conflict between two of his sons, Al-Amin and Al-Mamun, al-Rashid portioned the empire in two, leaving one chunk to each son. Al-Amin retained the title of caliph and the two sons retained a short-lived peace. However, due to political provocations of members of al-Amin’s court, Al-Mamun retaliated, resulting in a civil war[4]. Al-Amin’s death during this conflict resulted in Al-Mamun being anointed the seventh Abbasid caliph in 818 AD.

In most current literature, the duration of the reign of Al-Mamun is described as the time when the Abbasid intellectual movement rose to its apex. His rule is perhaps most famous for the vast amount of translation of Greek work into Arabic. These transcripts were mostly obtained through trade with the Byzantine Empire[5]. Al-Mamun’s reign is a highly unique time period to study the movement. Several important fiscal and political decisions of the Caliph’s government as well as a shifting cultural overtone make it ripe with room for analysis of the development of various intellectual movements and their interactions with the society that hosted them.

The Foundations of Islamic Mathematics

Mathematics was one of the myriads of subjects that saw intense development during this Islamic Renaissance. Mathematicians during the early medieval period drew upon the translated works of the Greek and Indian mathematics as their theoretical backbones. One of the most prolific and widely utilized set of Greeks works was Euclid’s Elements[6] . Although the 15 books of the set were supposedly sent to Caliph al-Mansur via the Byzantine emperor, the earliest known translation of the books carried out in the early ninth century, during the reign of Al-Mamun[7]. The books served as a provided the axiomatic foundations for a closer study of geometry, including the parallel postulate, definitions of proportions, and quadratic irrationalities. However, in addition to the inheritance in the form of practical lemmata, the Elements left Islamic mathematicians both with a skeletal structure by which they could format logical proofs and assertions that they could go about debating. This powerful combination sparked an era of logical discourse in the caliphate, with famous Arab polymaths like Umar al-Khayyam and Thabit ibn Qurrah writing treatises on the identity and inequalities of ratios[8]. The exact pattern of transmission of knowledge between India and the Abbasids is less straight forward than in the case of the Greeks. The complex economic relationships spurring from the Abbasid caliphate’s nature as an empire of commerce meant that there were many possible channels of flow of knowledge including Indians present in government courts, raids by traders and soldiers along trading routes or even travel of Islamic intellectuals to the Indian subcontinent[9]. Although translation of works directly from Sanskrit was most common, some works labelled as “Indian” were also translated from Pahlavi (Iranian) script, implying further complex connections between the two locales[10]. In addition to Indian trigonometry found in the Aryabhatiya (astronomical treatise developed by mathematician Aryabhata), there were two other important contributions to the field of mathematics by Indian mathematics. The first was the invention of the symbolic form of “zero”. Although the concept of nothingness or the absence of anything was not entirely novel philosophically or mathematically, it seems that the Indians were the first to represent zero in a numerical form[11]. Indian mathematician Brahmagupta in his famed work Brahmasphutasiddhanta manipulates the concept of zero in expressions with addition, subtraction and negative numbers and states various properties of zero[12] . This concept of zero becomes integral in the solving of different algebraic equations and in the development of the second important Indian mathematical contribution ­– the positional decimal numeral system. This system, with symbols from zero to nine that is the current global standard numerical system, served as an important turning point in the world-wide history of mathematics. This base-10 system allowed for a a distinctively much faster method of computation, serving another impetus of mathematical development in the Abbasid empire. It was popularized in Europe through Latin translations of Arabic works that used it.

Adding to the rich genealogy of Islamic mathematics during the reign of Al-Mamun was Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi (henceforth referred to as Al-Khwarizmi). The last part of his name, referred to in Arabic as the nisbah, a geographical signifier, implies that he was born in Khwarizm, a region located in present-day Uzbekistan. The middle of his name, the nasab, signifies that Al-Khwarizmi was the son of Musa.  This is a name of Islamic origin, but Khwarizm had only faced mass conversion to Islam a few years before the birth of Al-Khwarizmi (in 783 AD), so many historians speculate that he had Zoroastrian or Persian Jewish heritage[13] . Although his full name provides interesting insight into his heritage, it is also the Latinized version of the last part of this name that has turned into an omnipresent modern term: al-Khwarizmi, translated to algorizmus, misread as algoritmus, and known today, as algorithm[14].

Like most Abbasid-era intellectuals, al-Khwarizmi in no way limited his work to the field of mathematics. He was well-versed and published works in the fields of astronomy, imperial history, and geography[15]. Despite the robust nature of his expertise, al-Khwarizmi derives his contemporary fame from his association with the foundation of the mathematical branch of algebra.

Al-Jabr: Creation and Contents

In addition to analysis, geometry, combinatorics, and topology, algebra is one of the major divisions of pure mathematics. The name of this branch is almost directly derived from the title of al-Khwarizmi’s liminal work: kitaab al-muhtasar fi hisab al-jabr wa’l-muqabalah, written in around 820[16]. The exact translation of this title is posited to be “An Abridged Treatise on the jabr and muqabalah (types of) Calculation”[17]. The exact definitions of jabr and muqabalah are contended by historian-translators. The roots of the words in Arabic imply restore/compel and confrontation respectively and are used by al-Khwarizmi throughout his set of works to denote specific types of mathematical operations[18]. Contemporaries of al-Khwarizmi, like al-Khayyam and al-Hassar mostly referred to the novel method of calculation by al-jabr [19]. In Latin translations of the book, the title is transliterated to algebra et amuchabala[20] (Oaks). Later Italian spellings became argibra, aliabraa, alcibra, which transformed into the modern-day algebra[21].

            In addition to the many Latin and Italian translations of Al-jabr made during in the medieval period, there is one well-known English translations of al-Khwarizmi’s work. The translation was made in 1831 by Frederic Rosen in 1831, using an original Arabic manuscript kept in the Bodleian collection at Oxford[22]. This translation is the one referred through in this essay.

In the first, he posits that all linear and quadratic equations can be reduced to six variations and presents algorithms of solutions to each example for each of these types. Mathematical problems of similar algebraic nature were not entirely novel to Al-Khwarizmi’s work. Arithmetic, geometry as well as algebra were all explored in Babylonian and ancient Egyptian work as early as 2000 BC[24]. Hellenistic mathematician Diophantus, in his famed work Arithmetica, also discusses algebraic ideas, but his analysis focused more on the properties of the numbers in the equation[25]. The structure of the logical arguments in Al-Khwarizmi’s work does make it clear that he was a student of classical mathematics, but there is little evidence to suggest that is not original work[26]. What makes Al-jabr unique from the mathematics before it was its treatment of “the root” as quantity in its own right instead of a placeholder for some number[27].This unique treatment gave way to the foundation of algebra as a discrete branch of mathematics, different from the number theory of Diophantus or the geometry of Euclid [28]

            Although the first half of al-jabr definitively serves as the theoretical foundation of the work’s fame in modern times, it’s second half is no less important in understanding its profound nature. The second part is an anthology of examples of the applications of algebraic ideas that Al-Khwarizmi develops in the first half. Areas of discussion include accounting measurement, finance, dowries and seemingly most important, in terms of length discussed, inheritance laws[29]. The following is one of the shortest example situations enumerated in the inheritance chapter of the second part:

A man dies, and leaves four sons, and bequeaths to someone a dirhem, and as much as the share of a son, and one-fourth of what remains from one-third after the deduction of that share (Al-Khwarizmi, 116).

            The long-lived legacy of Al-Khwarizmi’s work is in some ways due to its translation into Latin by Gerard of Cremona, Robert of Chester and William of Luna[30] . These translations were used in mathematical education for centuries after they were published[31].  However, Al-jabr’s acceptance into the Western mathematical canon is far from its sole legacy. In the late tenth century, historian bibliographer Ibn al-Nadim chronicled a famous bibliography detailing the development of Islamic scientific knowledge titled al-Fihrist (The Catalogue in English)[32]. Al-Nadim devoted a section to listing the ideological descendants of Al-Khwarizmi’s work, of which there are many. Abu Kamil and Thabit ibn Qurra proved the geometric isomorphism between Al-jabr and Euclid’s Elements [33]. In the eleventh century, Omar al-Khayyam utilized Al-Khwarizmi’s work in pieces that later became foundational in the mathematical field of analysis[34] (ibid). Succinctly put, Al-jabr served as a critical jumping off point for the development of both Western and Eastern mathematical inquiry.

            The analysis of Al-jabr provided enough should be justification of its importance and relevance during the Islamic Renaissance of the Abbasid Empire. However, in order to gain a finer understanding of the intricate connections between the construct of empire and the development of mathematical knowledge, there is a need for a broader contextualization of Al-Khwarizmi and is work within the socio-political realities of the Abbasid empire.  The preceding introduction to his work omits this kind of explanation, aside from showing the importance of the massive translation movement of classical texts. Luckily, Al-Khwarizmi’s biographical details, his involvement in important educational constructs along with the political and religious realities of Al-Mamun’s (al-Khwarizmi’s ruler’s) reign give ample ground for this kind of analysis.

Algebra in Empire

Educational Institutions

If one wished to examine the close relationship between knowledge and power, an educational system usually serves as a useful example. Although the educational systems in the Abbasid empire differ from its modern European imperial counterpart, it still provides an insightful nature into the way in which the intellectual and political realms were connected.

The smallest and most important unit within the larger Abbasid educational framework was the madrasa (literally school in Arabic). These institutions were primarily focused the education of younger men in the fields of Islamic religion and its associated jurisprudence, and secondarily with subjects like mathematics, astronomy, etc.[35]  Throughout the beginning of the Islamic medieval period, these madrasas became “typical features of the urban landscapes of Near Eastern and central and southwest Asian cities”[36]. However, their roots were not necessarily strictly of Abbasid origin. At the start of the Umayyad dynasty, expansion into non-Arabic speaking regions (like the cities Damascus, Kufa and Basra) lead to a need for a formalized educational structure, which took the form of primary school systems[37]. The Abbasids built on this pre-existing system, creating madrasas, institutions of higher learning beyond the primary level.  It was not uncommon for any students to travel long distances to receive their educations at these institutions[38]. Residential education was common, and tuition was usually free usually from private endowments provided to the madrasa[39]. The removal of this financial barrier allowed for a diverse set students to be educated in the system.

The development of these educational systems certainly make sense in the context of Islamic doctrine:

The Qur’an is full of exhortations to pursue knowledge (e.g. Q. 20:114); it proclaims the superiority in God’s eyes of those who have knowledge (e.g. Q. 58:11 and 39:9), but also emphasizes wisdom and guidance rather than the blind acceptance of tradition (Q. 2:170, 17:36 and 6:148). The pursuit of knowledge, it seems, is a religious duty (Halstead, 520)

 

However, the rising importance of madrasas during the medieval period is not completely devoid of a practical or political nature. As mentioned before, the schools usually operated on the money of private benefactors. Each madrasa was usually founded by singular individuals, who were often foreign-born[40].  Assigning their names to these institutions bought these individuals societal attention as well as buying them credibility as faithful Muslims, providing them with increased political capital[41]. The foundation of madrasas “helped to confer legitimacy on military regimes which operated in varying degrees of cultural tension with the indigenous Muslim populations over whom they ruled”[42]. Thus, the instability and diversity inherent to empire had a large hand in the creation of these enterprises of knowledge.

Considering this trend, it is highly pertinent to note: “ruling elites left the institutions largely untouched and left the ‘ulama [scholars] with a generally free hand to supervise and regulate the transmission of knowledge itself”[43]. The Abbasid empire became a place where

knowledge proves to be for the first time the only credible alternative by means of which the political body can effectively justify its rule. As a result of the vulnerability of the political power due to the conditional support of the legal authority, the emerging distinctive political and social configuration is that the body politics finds its rule dependent on its unlimited support for knowledge and not knowledge which should rely on the goodwill of the politicians (Rahman, 22).

 

That is, aside from the political gains of name-brand association, there was an important lack of systematic or functional links between the governments and the various madrasas.  Unlike many modern colonial and imperial educational structures, where curricular decisions are made by some central body to serve specific imperial functions, the Abbasid educational system was remarkably more personal and informal. In its avoidance of formal curriculum, examination and hierarchy, the madrasa system achieved the creation of a remarkably open elite. Thus, the intricate relationship between power and education in the Abbasid context was complicated. The nature of knowledge as proximate to religion allowed the ruling elite to maintain a spiritual sort of power in their control of the transmission of knowledge. However, that does not mean that power was wielded with any specific change in mind. In reality, the inherent characterization of the Islamic educational system, as ordained by the Qur’an, was conservative, acting as a force of stability rather than of change[44]. The lack of oversight a formal authoritative body (perhaps a direct consequence of the aforementioned conservative nature of education) allowed for unique and unregulated inquiry into a variety of subjects performed by a heavily heterogenous set of minds.

The specific subject in question, Al-Khwarizmi, undoubtedly benefited from this important part of Abbasid societal infrastructure. As mentioned before, although few details of al-Khwarizmi’s life are known with certainty, some historians have speculated about the Persian man’s potential Jewish or Zoroastrian heritage. Despite his potential non-Arab and non-Muslim heritage, his geographic region of origin, Khwarizm (located around 2000 kilometers away from Baghdad) and his lack of any recorded ties to the imperial elite, Al-Khwarizmi was still able to access the educational foundation that lead to his subsequent success. Reportedly, Al-Khwarizmi received his education at prominent madrasas in the modern-day cities of Khiva, Kiat and Gurgench[45]. Despite the lack of clarity about his upbringing, it can safely be posited that Al-Khwarizmi’s primary education must have been of high quality, because by the beginning of the ninth century, he became a well-respected name at one of the most well-known educational institutions in the empire – the House of Wisdom, and had a direct connection to Caliph Al-Mamun.

Al-Mamun and Realpolitik

Although the connection between government and madrasas gave important insight into one of the ways in which empire and knowledge were connected, this trend alone does not shed light on the full picture of the connection. Specifically, the reign of Caliph Al-Mamun provides interesting areas of analysis, because of personality of the Caliph himself and because of specific political and religious reform that went on during its duration.

Interestingly, Al-Khwarizmi’s educational youth coincided with Al-Mamun reign as governor of the region of Khorasan (which contained Khwarizm), which was the position he held before ascending to the throne[46] . Although it is unknown when they made their initial contact, Al-Mamun must have become aware of Al-Khwarizmi’s capabilities, as the latter later came to head the bayt al-hikmah, famously known as Al-Mamun’s Baghdadi-located pet project. The official beginnings of the bayt al-hikmah (The House of Wisdom in English) are hypothesized to have been in the early eighth century, under the reign of Caliph Al-Mansur[47] . At first, its primary function was modelled after that of a royal library, required to the accommodate the vast number of classical and foreign works being translated and studied [48]. However, although the House of Wisdom has come, in modern times, to represent a physical symbol of the Abbasid Enlightenment, in reality very little detail it known about its true nature, its real organizational structure, or the work that was done within it in the time of Al-Mamun[49] . This is not to say that there was not robust and lively intellectual activity in the city of Baghdad. Debates, lectures and assemblies were common occurrences in city programming[50]. However, the grouping of all this activity under the umbrella of one organization seems to have been a modern invention. Despite the shadowy details, the bayt al-hikmah serves as useful construct in which to examine the direct connections between Al-Khwarizmi and Al-Mamun. One of the most widely cited primary sources in studies of the bayt al-hikmah is the previously mentioned al-Fihrist by Al-Nadim, who mentions explicitly that Al-Khwarizmi was employed full-time at the bayt al-hikmah in the service of al-Mamun[51]. In fact, the introduction to Al-jabr contains explicit mention of the Caliph:

That fondness for science, by which God has distinguished the Imam al-Mamun, that affability and condescension which he shows to the learned, that promptitude with which he protects and supports them in the elucidation of obscurities and in the removal of difficulties—has encouraged me to compose a short work on Calculating by (the rules of) Completion and Reduction, confining it to what is easiest and most useful in arithmetic, such as men constantly require in cases of inheritance, legacies, partition, lawsuits, and trade, and in all their dealing with one another (Al-Khwarizmi, 4).

If taken literally, it seems that Al-Mamun’s respect and dedication to supporting the endeavors of scientists had earned him Al-Khwarizmi’s praise. Additionally, historians interpret this introduction as signifying that Al-Mamun specifically “requested the book and asked that it cover ‘what is easiest and most useful’ in algebra”[52]. This makes it clear that the relationship between imperial power and the creation of knowledge is more intimate than the analysis in the previous subsection might imply. Al-Mamun seemed to have a large hand in the creation of Al-jabr as a work and especially in the highly applied nature of its second part. However, although providing imperial citizens with mathematical assistance in approaching difficult laws might have provided utility in growing or maintaining popularity, it does not seem likely that Al-Mamun tried to specifically morph the knowledge created to benefit himself. This can be attributed to the sanctity of knowledge within Islam as described in the previous section.

            Even if Al-Mamun did not use the specifics of the mathematical discovery directly as an imperial tool, it is still important to examine the reasons for his heavy patronage of intellectual movement.  Some part of this inclination may have been due to his own status as an intellectual. Al-Nadim reports of Al-Mamun, “We are too rich in famed traditions concerning him to go into detail when mentioning him”[53]. Al-Mamun reportedly memorized the Qur’an during his youth, a serious endeavor during that time, displaying high intellectual tenacity[54]. He was also well-versed in Hellenistic traditions and philosophy as a young man[55]. His intelligence was recognized outside the border of the Caliphate, as Christian-Syrian Bishop Abu Qurrah reportedly said of Al-Mamun, “When the renowned philosopher converses with al-Mamun, incapacity of speech dries up his tongue”[56]. Al-Nadim also writes a lengthy account of one of Al-Mamun’s dreams, in which he has a lengthy conversation with Aristotle[57]. Aristotle apparently confirmed Al-Mamun’s thoughts that “scientific scholarship was a religious duty”[58].

            Dreams aside, Al-Mamun’s support for the explosion of intellectual development was surely somewhat rooted in his own intelligence and curiosity. However, as is usually case, the motivations are still more complicated. It is important to recall that Al-Mamun inherited the title of Caliph after a lengthy civil war struggle that ended with the death of his brother Al-Amin. The foundations of his reign on regicide/fratricide as well as political unrest that had started to rise during his father’s reign lead to a need for Al-Mamun to take action to legitimize his rule and unify his people. These actions took two main forms.

            The first was the promotion of an anti-Byzantine and anti-Christian narrative. The shift towards an aggressive policy against the Byzantines allowed Al-Mamun to “present himself as the champion of Islam” [59].  Essentially, the narrative was presented in the following way

The Byzantines were portrayed as deserving of Muslim attacks not only because they were infidels…but because they were also culturally benighted and inferior… to Muslims [who] in addition to being superior because of Islam, were also superior because they appreciated ancient Greek science and wisdom (Gutas, 84)

 

The portrayal of Islam as the “rational” religion was not only promoted by the government, but also adopted into the writings of intellectuals, who used the stance to advocate for the benefits of translation and development of scientific practices, simultaneously while Am-Mamun used it to maintain control[60].

This outwardly focused policy was also accompanied by domestic changes, particularly, Al-Mamun’s institution of the mihna.(literally ordeal in English). The mihna was an officially sanctioned period of inquisition meant to enforce (sometimes violently) the practice of the Islamic school of Mu’tazilism. The school, in addition to non-traditional views on the longevity of the Qu’ran, was defined by its “rationalist” tendency. It is important to note that modern labels of Mu’tazilism as “rationalist” and “free-thinking” often serve an Orientalist view of Islam[61]. Keeping this in mind, the “rationalist” nature of Mu’tazilism was still conservative in its adherence to Islamic scripture, and instead encouraged the use of reason and discourse to examine the basic dogmas of Islam[62]. The theological ethic of the Mu’tazila emerged in the context of the immense diversity of the Abbasid empire, as “many of the newly converted Muslims shared in a more Hellenistic world view, and incorporated techniques of Hellenistic rational argumentation into polemics with the intelligentsia of other faiths”[63]. It is perhaps this inherently universalizing characteristic of Mu’tazilism that made it a forceful tool of imperial nature for the Caliphate. Regardless, it was clear that the minha was “necessary to maintain the image of the caliph as champion of Islam, strengthen his religious and hence political authority and… weaken the religious authority of the leaders among the masses who might have ‘concealed leaderships’”[64]. In addition to the forceful persecution of observers of other schools of Islam, the promotion of Mu’tazilism, which called for the use of logic as one of its basic principles, required the “fostering [of] an intellectual elite that would conduct discussion” and the “sponsorship of intellectual gatherings… where the dialectic method of argumentation reigned supreme”[65]. In order to deter any discrepancies between his actions and the religious believes he so heavily promoted, Al-Mamun probably turned to the patronage of intellectual activity to strengthen the ideological basis of the minha. The cultivation of his image as leader of an empire with a “rationalist” faith seems successful as seen by the introductions in Al-jabr and his portrayal in official documents of state history like Al-Nadim’s Al-Fihrist. Thus, the connection between the caliphate and the promotion of intellectual activity became more palpable, in a very realpolitik sense.

Conclusions

Mathematics is often described as a “masculine” discipline. This description is not meant to tie it to a biological category, but rather to assign the disciples the traits of “objectivity, separation, control of knower over nature and a strictly intellectual way of knowing that denies other ways of knowing” [66].  In line with this characterization of the discipline is the characterization of the study of the history of this discipline. The history of mathematics tends to be “technical, to focus on the content of specific papers” or “the antecedents of certain mathematical developments”[67]. It is mostly an insular practice. This makes sense to some extent given mathematics’ nature highly genealogical discipline. However, considering the discipline’s powerful ability to bridge the gap between highly theoretical metaphysics and tangible applications, it seems imprudent not to use it as a tool to examine societal constructs, especially ones that depend on the fusing on ideology and execution – like empire.

The study of some kinds of epistemologies in the context of empire are not particularly novel. In fact, the colonialism of the imperial West “played a crucial role in denying or subduing the contributions made by the Arabs… to development of mathematics”[68]. Twentieth century European, especially British, imperialism provides fertile ground for examples of the use of knowledge as a tool of empire:

British natural science in the nineteenth century conquered new lands by reducing them to regularity and providing the requisite information to exploit them. Its importation of overseas data into libraries and museums of the metropolis constituted in one sense a gigantic looting operation which helped maintain British ascendancy. British geology and geography, as well as other sciences were significantly influenced by Britain’s possession of a colonial empire. (Bennett, 7).

It seems to be that sustenance and growth are two primary concerns of empire. In the British context, these goals are worked towards with the under the guise of progress. In the more explicit work, like the development of navigational tools and transportation technology, the connection is easier to see. However, imperialism also persisted in a more insidious manner through the likes of institutions of higher education, museums, and through the creation of the academic elite[69].

Analysis of fields like cartography or ecology in relation to imperial tendencies have been robust and common probably due to their evident connections to practical usage. Mathematics, however, especially in its purest essence, has been always been typically viewed as “objective and indifferent to human concerns”. Certainly, with its use of structures without real world analogs and its internal logic instead out outwards empiricism, mathematics does lay claim to its label as the inquiry of truth. However, like any kind of inquiry, scientific, historical or other, mathematical inquiry is still a human-conducted activity. As such, mathematical objects are “a variety of social-historical objects”, a special part of culture, but a part of culture nonetheless[70]. Where mathematics is perhaps most vulnerable in being subject to analysis as a “social-historical object” is in its application. It is commonly referred to as the language of the sciences. Deep philosophical questions about free will and the creation of the universe owe much to the applicability of mathematics. Even outside the realm of the “hard sciences”, Comte’s construction of logical positivism was reportedly inspired by his studies of Lagrange’s attempts to reduce all of mechanics to mathematical facts in Analytical Mechanics[71]. The ability of the mathematical discipline to provide a theoretically universal language gives it an important weight as a useful and unifying tool.

It is here that the connection back to Abbasid Caliphate and Al-Khwarizmi can be made. Considering ­Al-Jabr­’s nature as being a work requested specifically by the Caliph, in order to provide mathematical guidance on issues important to most citizens of the Caliphate, there is a generally clear connection between the foundations of one of the most important branches of mathematics and socio-cultural need. In the British case, the tool of scientific inquiry was used to aid the elitism and large-scale exploitation that sustained the empire. Contrastingly, though probably not uniquely, the Abbasid Caliphate’s imperial strategy at least partly relied on the adaption of the government to gain approval of the populous instead of the other way around, and the development of mathematical knowledge fit perfectly into this paradigm. The pliability of mathematics of Al-Jabr and the universalizing nature of education that gave rise to its existence were tools wielded by Al-Mamun to maintain imperial power. When considering the socio-political realities of the context of Al-jabr, Mathematics seemed to be emphatically connected to culture when considering the practical and epistemological dogma of Islamic that it was molded into in the Abbasid era. This is not to imply a unidirectional relationship between mathematics, religion and the structures of the Caliphate. The connections between them were and remain steadfastly complicated. However, the evident relationship among the three during Al-Mamun’s reign, even if complicated, provides important insight. If mathematics is thought to play a part in defining some version of reality, then certainly, with empire as an ever-present construct in human history, it accomplishes this goal.
